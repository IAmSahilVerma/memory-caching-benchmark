Memory Caching vs Transformer

A sequence recall benchmark replicating the core claim of the Google Research / Cornell Memory Caching paper (ICLR 2026 submission) at small scale.

This benchmark compares four models — a GRU baseline, a GRU augmented with Residual Memory Caching (RMC), a GRU augmented with Gated Residual Memory Caching (GRMC), and a small Transformer — across three tasks designed to stress different aspects of sequence recall: Selective Copy, Associative Recall, and Language Modelling on WikiText-2. The Transformer serves as the upper-bound reference, and the plain GRU serves as the lower-bound reference. The central question is whether Memory Caching, with no architectural change beyond cached-state injection, meaningfully closes the gap between the two.

Methodology note. Two issues were caught and corrected during this benchmark. First, an early version gave the Transformer roughly 3.8× more parameters than the GRU variants on the synthetic tasks; results below use parameter-matched models (within 0.3% of each other) so the comparison reflects architecture, not model size. Second, an initial run used a higher learning rate for the GRU variants than the Transformer to work around a slow training start; this run uses one learning rate (3e-4) and one optimizer configuration for all four models. On Selective Copy specifically, the GRU variants needed 80 epochs rather than 40 to reach a converged result under this stricter, shared learning rate — the Transformer converges within its first 20 epochs and is shown at 40. The line chart for that task reflects this directly: the Transformer's curve ends at epoch 40, while the GRU variants continue to epoch 80.

Summary

Final-epoch results across all three tasks. Selective Copy and Associative Recall report validation accuracy on the recall positions (higher is better). Language Modelling reports validation perplexity, the standard metric for comparing language models (lower is better).

Model Selective Copy (acc) Associative Recall (acc) Language Modelling (perplexity)

Selective Copy

The model sees a sequence of random tokens with a handful of scattered "marked" values. After a copy marker, it must reproduce those values in order, autoregressively — each recalled value is fed back in to predict the next. This tests whether a model's hidden state can preserve specific content from anywhere in a long sequence, not just recent positions.

Associative Recall

Key-value pairs are presented early in the sequence; a single query key appears at the end, and the model must output the value originally paired with it. Unlike Selective Copy, this requires genuine key-to-value lookup rather than positional memorization — widely considered the harder of the two synthetic recall tasks for fixed-memory models.

Language Modelling

Standard next-token prediction on WikiText-2, tokenized with GPT-2's BPE tokenizer. This grounds the synthetic recall results in a real-world signal: does better recall ability actually translate into better natural language modelling, measured by perplexity?

Takeaways

With parameters and learning rate controlled across all four models, the results tell a more honest and more textured story than the first pass at this benchmark did.

On Selective Copy, once every GRU variant was given enough training to actually converge, GRMC is the clear winner among the recurrent models at 25.3% final accuracy, ahead of the plain GRU baseline (24.1%) and well ahead of RMC (18.3%). Notably, RMC's validation loss was still climbing in its final ten epochs even as its accuracy plateaued, a sign of mild instability that neither the baseline nor the gated variant showed. Taken together, this is a reasonably clean empirical case for the paper's central design choice: an ungated cache is not just weaker than a gated one, it can be measurably less stable to train. The Transformer remains far ahead of all three (99.2%), which is expected given its full-sequence attention.

On Associative Recall, the ordering shifted under the stricter, shared learning rate: RMC (8.75%) outperforms both the plain GRU baseline (3.75%) and GRMC (3.5%), the opposite of the Selective Copy ordering. Every recurrent model remains far behind the Transformer (11.25%), and all results are close to or only modestly above the 3.1% chance baseline. This task remains the hardest and least conclusive of the three at this model scale and training budget, and the fact that the RMC/GRMC ordering flips between tasks is itself worth treating as a real finding rather than noise to explain away: at this scale, which caching variant helps most appears to depend on the nature of the recall required, not on which closes the gap to the Transformer more reliably.

On Language Modelling, the ordering is stable across this version and the earlier run: the plain GRU baseline still achieves the lowest perplexity (~294), narrowly ahead of GRMC (~301) and RMC (~312), with the Transformer highest (~500). This likely reflects that natural language perplexity rewards general statistical fluency more than long-range recall at this scale, so the extra caching machinery doesn't show an advantage here, and may carry a marginal training cost.

Overall, this small-scale replication supports the paper's claim about gating only partially and only on the task most directly suited to it. The clearest, most defensible result is that GRMC outperforms RMC on Selective Copy under matched conditions; everywhere else, results are closer, noisier, or favour the simpler baseline. That mixed picture is consistent with the paper's own framing that the gains from Memory Caching scale with model and training budget; at the toy scale used here (roughly 800K to 1.5M GRU parameters, 40 to 80 epochs, single-task synthetic data), the mechanism shows real promise on one task and an inconclusive or negative result on the other two.

Built as a portfolio replication of the Memory Caching paper's core recall-vs-efficiency claim, at academic-hobbyist scale (small models, single consumer GPU). Not an attempt to reproduce the paper's frontier-scale results.
